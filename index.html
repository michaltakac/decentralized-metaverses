The Genesis: Early Virtual Worlds

1970s-1980s

1970s: MUDs

Text-based multi-user dungeons on ARPANET

1986: Lucasfilm's Habitat

First graphical virtual world with:

  • • Thousands of players as cartoon avatars
  • • Virtual currency and items
  • • User-driven content and emergent economies
  • • Persistent world changes
"Don't bother writing AIs, just let real people interact"
— Chip Morningstar, Habitat creator

Virtual Worlds in the 1990s

From Text to 3D

Active Worlds (1995)

  • User-owned virtual land
  • Multi-world architecture
  • First virtual weddings (1996)
  • Still running today!

The Palace (1995)

  • 2D graphical chat rooms
  • User-customizable avatars
  • Peak cultural moment
  • Influenced later social apps

Neal Stephenson coins "Metaverse" in Snow Crash (1992)

- Active Worlds proved longevity of user-generated worlds - Multiple platforms experimented with different approaches - Term "metaverse" enters the lexicon

The Modern Metaverse: Second Life

2003-Present

Revolutionary Features

  • User-generated everything (2B+ items created)
  • L$ ↔ USD convertibility
  • In-world scripting (LSL)
  • Virtual land ownership as a service

Economic Impact

  • Peak: 88,000 concurrent users
  • 2009: $567M user-to-user transactions
  • 2023: $650M annual GDP
  • 14 users cashed out $1M+ in 2022

First realization of the metaverse vision:
Open-ended digital world with its own economy and culture

Scaling Solutions & Trade-offs

The Blockchain Trilemma in Practice

Ethereum L1

  • ✓ Maximum security
  • ✓ Deepest liquidity
  • ✗ High gas costs ($5-50/tx)
  • ✗ 15 TPS limit

Polygon (Sidechain)

  • ✓ Low fees (<$0.01)
  • ✓ EVM compatible
  • ✗ Weaker security
  • ~ Used by DCL/Sandbox

Layer 2 Rollups: The Future

Optimistic (Arbitrum, Optimism)

Cheap, 7-day withdrawals

ZK (zkSync, StarkNet)

Fast finality, complex proofs

EIP-4844 (2024): 10-100x cheaper L2 transactions! (blob field in transactions for larger data payloads)
